The Essence of Zorig Chusum

Zorig Chusum translates to "The Thirteen Crafts" and represents Bhutan's traditional arts and crafts that date back centuries. Established as an integral part of Bhutanese culture, these arts were formally categorized during the reign of the 4th Temporal Ruler, Tenzing Rabgye (1680-1694).

Each of these crafts plays a vital role in Bhutan's cultural identity, passed down through generations and still thriving today in monasteries, artisan workshops, and vocational schools across the country.

The Thirteen Traditional Arts of Bhutan

Click on each art to learn about its history, techniques, and contemporary significance in Bhutanese society.

Lhadri - Thangka Painting

Sacred Buddhist scroll paintings depicting deities and mandalas.

Thangka paintings serve as important meditation tools for Buddhist practitioners. Using natural pigments, artists follow strict iconometric guidelines passed down over centuries. The process includes priming cotton canvas with chalk and animal glue before painting with brushes made from animal hair.

Shingzo - Woodworking

Sacred wood carving for temples and traditional architecture.

Bhutanese woodworkers create intricate designs for dzongs, temples, and homes using hand tools. The craft includes carving religious symbols, floral patterns, and mythical creatures. Traditional Bhutanese architecture features elaborately carved windows, doors, and pillars that demonstrate exceptional craftsmanship.

Parzo - Stone Carving

Art of carving stone into sacred objects and architecture.

Bhutanese stone carvers create Buddhist prayer stones (mani stones), water fountains, and architectural elements. The most common types inscribed are the six-syllabled mantra of Avalokiteshvara (Om Mani Padme Hum) and other religious texts. These stones can be found lining pathways and building walls throughout Bhutan.

Lhazo - Ornamental Painting

Decorative painting of temples and religious artifacts.

This art encompasses the decorative painting of temples, houses, and artifacts with intricate floral designs, religious symbols, and auspicious motifs. The colors are made from natural mineral and vegetable pigments. Lhazo artists must master specific color combinations and patterns that carry deep symbolic meaning in Bhutanese culture.

Jimzo - Clay Arts

Sculpting religious statues and pottery.

Jimzo includes both sacred sculpture and utilitarian pottery. Religious clay sculptures (tsha-tsha) are small votive tablets often placed in stupas. Larger clay statues adorn temples. Potters also create household items using traditional techniques. Certain clay formations are believed to have medicinal properties in Bhutanese folk traditions.

Garzo - Blacksmithing

Forging iron implements and religious items.

Bhutanese blacksmiths produce tools, weapons, and religious objects. The craft holds spiritual significance as blacksmiths are believed to have special powers to work with "earth metal." Traditional products include knives (patag), axes, bells, chains, and ritual objects like phurbas (ceremonial daggers). The iron ore is locally sourced and processed in small forges.

Tsharzo - Bamboo Craft

Weaving baskets and household items from bamboo.

Tsharzo involves creating a variety of items from bamboo and cane including baskets (bangchung), mats, containers, bows, arrows, and even traditional hats. Different regions have distinctive weaving patterns. The craft is particularly important in rural communities where bamboo products remain essential for daily life, though artisans are now creating more decorative items for the tourist market.

Dozo - Masonry

Traditional Bhutanese stone construction.

Dozo refers to the traditional construction of stone buildings, primarily dzongs (fortress monasteries) and houses. Bhutanese masons build without mortar, using precise stone placement and wooden pins for stability. This technique has proven remarkably resilient to earthquakes. Characteristics include inward-sloping walls (batter), whitewashed surfaces with red ochre bands, and distinctive wooden roofs.

Trozo - Silver & Gold Smithing

Crafting jewelry and religious items.

Bhutanese gold and silversmiths create jewelry, ritual objects, and decorative items. Traditional work includes filigree, repoussé, and granulation techniques. Common items include amulet boxes (gau), which hold sacred relics, religious figurines, and jewelry featuring traditional motifs. Gold is considered sacred in Bhutan and used primarily for religious artifacts.

Tshemzo - Embroidery

Needlework for clothing and religious artifacts.

Tshemzo encompasses both secular and religious embroidery. It's used to decorate clothing (especially the kabney - ceremonial scarves), altar cloths, and temple hangings. Designs often feature the eight auspicious symbols, floral patterns, and mythical animals. Special appliqué techniques are used to create large thangkas made of silk, which serve as backdrops for religious ceremonies.

Lugzo - Bronze Casting

Creating bronze statues and ritual objects.

Lugzo artists use the lost-wax (cire perdue) method to cast bronze statues, ritual objects, and temple bells. The sculptures range from small amulets to massive Buddha statues several meters tall. The craft is deeply connected to religious practice, with the casting process often accompanied by rituals. Bronze was historically imported from neighboring countries via trade routes through the Himalayas.

Dhezho - Paper Making

Traditional paper from Daphne bark.

Bhutanese paper is made from the bark of Daphne trees (Daphne cannabina and Daphne bholua) using a process that dates back centuries. The paper is valued for its durability and insect-resistant properties. Traditionally used for religious texts, it's now fashioned into lampshades, stationery, and decorative items. The production process is labor-intensive and completely manual.

Tshemdrup - Tailoring

Crafting the iconic Bhutanese national dress.

Tshemdrup involves sewing Bhutan's national dress: the gho for men and kira for women. The gho is a knee-length robe tied with a belt, while the kira is a long rectangular piece of fabric wrapped around the body. The art requires intricate hand-sewing for traditional occasions, though machine stitching is now common for everyday wear. Tailors must master specific folding and fitting techniques unique to Bhutanese garments.
